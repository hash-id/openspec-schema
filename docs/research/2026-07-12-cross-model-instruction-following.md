# How well do Claude, GLM, and MiniMax follow schema.yaml-style long structured instructions, and what prompt engineering measurably helps?

**Date:** 2026-07-12
**Scope:** research how Claude Sonnet 4.6 / Claude Haiku 4.5 (Anthropic), GLM-4.7 (Zhipu AI / Z.ai), and MiniMax M2.7 (MiniMax AI) actually comply with long, structured, multi-step natural-language instructions — the pattern `openspec/schemas/hash/schema.yaml` uses throughout (numbered steps, capitalized MUST/DO NOT gates, HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW severity tiers, MECHANICAL/DECISION tagging, max-N-pass loops with early-stop, fresh-subagent review delegation) — plus model-agnostic findings on what prompt-engineering techniques measurably improve compliance. Pure research — no changes applied from this document.
**Trigger:** `hash`'s align and apply phases (schema.yaml lines 166–252) are exactly the failure-prone pattern this research targets: long instruction blocks (40–50 lines each), a bounded review loop with an early-stop condition, severity/tag-driven branching, and a hard requirement to run review in "a fresh agent session (subagent) with no memory of the implementer or prior passes" (line 220). Whether current models actually honor that shape under realistic prompt lengths is an empirical question this document tries to answer with evidence, not assumption.
**Research process note:** this document's research was gathered through a combination of direct WebSearch/WebFetch calls run in this session and results relayed from background research agents; some planned deep-dive angles (e.g. exhaustive Reddit thread-by-thread review for GLM/MiniMax) were cut short by session constraints. Source counts and gaps are reported honestly in §7 rather than padded.

## 1. Answer up front

All three model families can follow schema.yaml-length instructions (40–50 lines per phase, 250 lines total) reasonably well **on the easy parts** — linear numbered steps, single MUST gates near the start or end of a block — but every source that measures rather than asserts compliance shows **real, non-trivial degradation on exactly the patterns `hash` leans on hardest**: multi-constraint instructions (AGENTIF shows models drop 20–30 points from IFEval-style single-constraint scores once an instruction carries ~12 simultaneous constraints, which is roughly `hash`'s align/apply instruction density), buried-in-the-middle content ("lost in the middle" / "context rot" research shows 30+ point accuracy drops for information placed in the middle third of a long context, regardless of vendor), and self-review bias (same-session self-review catches measurably fewer injected errors than fresh-context review — direct empirical support for `hash`'s subagent-review requirement). Claude is the best-documented of the three for this specific failure class (Anthropic's own docs concede "lost in the middle" and recommend critical instructions go first/last, not the middle) and GLM/MiniMax are shipped and marketed specifically as agentic-coding models with genuinely strong SWE-bench/Terminal-Bench numbers, but both have open, version-matched GitHub issues (GLM-4.7 on sglang, MiniMax-M2.7 on vllm) showing tool-call-format breakage *specifically inside coding-agent tool-loop usage* — a different failure mode than instruction-following per se, but one that compounds it, since a malformed tool call in the middle of `hash`'s apply-phase TDD loop or align's `openspec validate --strict` check can silently break the gate it's supposed to enforce. Net effect for `hash`: the schema's existing patterns (fresh-subagent review, severity/tag classification, capped-loop-with-early-stop, sparse capitalized MUST) are already aligned with what the evidence says works; the residual risk is model-agnostic instruction density and mid-document placement, not a Claude-vs-GLM-vs-MiniMax difference big enough to warrant model-specific schema forks.

## 3. Claude Sonnet 4.6 / Haiku 4.5

### Official guidance on long prompts and instruction placement

- Anthropic's own **prompting-long-context** guidance is explicit about primacy/recency: "the importance of putting instructions at the end of the prompt should be emphasized, as we want Claude's recall of them to be as high as possible" — a direct, official acknowledgment that middle-of-document placement is the weak spot, not a community rumor. [anthropic.com/news/prompting-long-context]
- Practitioner syntheses of Claude Code's own system-prompt structure (reverse-engineered) describe a deliberate shape: identity/safety at the top (primacy), core workflow in the upper-middle, reminders at the bottom (recency) — i.e. Anthropic's own product avoids putting anything load-bearing in the middle third of its own long system prompt. [mynameisfeng.com blog on reverse-engineering Claude Code's system prompt]
- Claude Code's official best-practices docs favor **short, concise custom instructions** — "every line earns its place" — which is in tension with `hash`'s 40–50-line-per-phase instruction blocks; the tension isn't necessarily a problem (concision is a stated ideal, not a hard ceiling), but it's evidence the vendor's own guidance treats length itself as a cost to compliance, not a neutral variable.

### Subagents and fresh-context review

- Anthropic's official subagent documentation states the exact mechanism `hash`'s apply-REVIEW phase relies on: "Review subagents evaluate code without knowing what tradeoffs were considered, what approaches were rejected, or what assumptions were made... this outside perspective surfaces issues the main conversation might miss." Each subagent runs in its own fresh context; only its final message returns to the parent. [claude.com/blog/subagents-in-claude-code; code.claude.com/docs/en/agent-sdk/subagents]
- This is corroborated by independent research (§6) showing same-session self-review measurably underperforms fresh-context review on injected-error recall — Anthropic's product design and the academic literature converge on the same claim from different directions.

### Known failure modes

- "Lost in the middle" and "context rot" are not Claude-specific but Claude is explicitly included in the most-cited cross-model study (Chroma's Context Rot research tested 18 frontier models including Claude 4-generation models) — the finding that every model tested degrades with added length, and that placement in positions 5–15 of a 20-item context costs 30+ accuracy points versus first/last placement, applies to Claude as much as any other vendor tested. [research.trychroma.com/context-rot]
- Community/practitioner writing (dev.to) frames this for Claude Code specifically as "context drift": "as conversations grow, original instructions get pushed further from the active generation point, and the model's attention shifts toward recent context... this problem happens in every long coding session." The same piece argues CLAUDE.md-style standing instructions partially mitigate this because they're re-read at the start of every interaction rather than relied upon from deep conversation history — directly relevant to why `hash`'s schema.yaml instructions are re-injected per-phase rather than accumulated across one long session. [dev.to/olivia_craft]

### Benchmarks

- No IFEval/FollowBench/Multi-IF number specific to Sonnet 4.6 or Haiku 4.5 was found published by Anthropic directly in the system card excerpts retrieved; third-party aggregator BenchLM.ai reports Sonnet 4.6 ranked "#26 of 70 models... average instruction-following score of 79.9" on its own aggregate methodology — treat as a low-confidence secondary source, not a primary benchmark result, since the underlying test suite and scoring weights weren't independently verified in this research pass.
- Sonnet 4.6 reaches 79.6% SWE-bench Verified per Anthropic's own announcement — an agentic-coding benchmark where instruction adherence (following task specs, using tools correctly, respecting constraints) is a necessary component of the score, though SWE-bench doesn't isolate instruction-following as a separate metric.

### Gap acknowledgment

Version-matched, first-party quantitative instruction-following numbers (an actual IFEval or FollowBench score published by Anthropic for Sonnet 4.6/Haiku 4.5 specifically) were not found in this research pass — Anthropic's system cards emphasize safety evals and agentic/coding benchmarks over classic instruction-following suites. This is a real gap, not filled by inference from older Claude generations.

## 4. GLM-4.7

### Official positioning

- Z.ai markets GLM-4.7 explicitly for "real-world development environments," multi-step coding tasks, and terminal automation, with three named reasoning mechanisms relevant to instruction adherence: **Interleaved Thinking** (reasons before every response and tool call), **Preserved Thinking** (retains reasoning context across turns "to maintain consistency"), and **Turn-level Thinking** (toggle reasoning depth per turn). The "Preserved Thinking" claim is the most directly relevant to `hash`'s multi-pass align/apply loops, since it's a vendor claim about maintaining consistency across exactly the kind of multi-turn, multi-pass structure `hash` uses — but it's a marketing claim, not something independently verified against a long structured system prompt in this research pass. [prnewswire.com; techloy.com]
- GLM-4.6 (immediate predecessor, more independently documented) is described by third-party technical analysis as having improved tool-use reliability over GLM-4.5, "aiming for zero hallucination in tool calls," refusing unknown tools and minimizing invented arguments. [cirra.ai technical analysis]

### Benchmarks

- GLM-4.7: 84.9% LiveCodeBench, 73.8% SWE-bench Verified (open-weight-model high at release), 95.7% AIME 2025. A Hacker News thread reports a stricter SWE-bench Verified cut of 59.2%, versus Qwen3's 22% on the same cut — the discrepancy between the 73.8% and 59.2% figures likely reflects different evaluation harnesses/subsets and should be read as "strong but harness-dependent," not a contradiction to resolve here. [techloy.com; HN #46357287]
- GLM-4.6 (predecessor): CC-Bench (Zhipu's own real-world coding benchmark, human-evaluated, Docker sandboxed, 70+ tasks) shows GLM-4.6 winning ~48.6% of head-to-head evaluations against Claude Sonnet 4 — "near parity," per third-party summary, though this is Zhipu's own benchmark design, not a neutral third party. [marktechpost.com]
- No GLM-specific IFEval/FollowBench/Multi-IF score was found published by Zhipu or independently in this research pass. The AGENTIF benchmark (§6) — the most relevant instruction-following-in-agentic-scenarios benchmark found — does not appear to have published GLM-4.6/4.7 results in the sources retrieved.

### Community and GitHub-issue evidence (the most concrete finding for GLM)

- **sglang issue #15721** ("[Bug] GLM 4.7 tool calling error in Claude Code"): SGLang's streaming tool-call parser for GLM-4.7 (`glm47_moe_detector.py`) crashes with `AttributeError: 'NoneType' object has no attribute 'strip'` when a malformed or duplicated `<tool_call>` tag appears — and the bug report explicitly states the crash happens **"while using a coding agent (e.g., Claude Code) for repository editing/refactoring, where the agent repeatedly invokes file tools and the model response is streamed."** This is a version-matched (GLM-4.7), context-matched (coding-agent tool loop) real-world failure report, not a synthetic benchmark — the single strongest piece of GLM-specific evidence found in this research. [github.com/sgl-project/sglang/issues/15721]
- **sglang issue #11888**: GLM-4.6 tool calls don't stream argument tokens incrementally under SGLang — arguments arrive as one large blocking chunk instead of progressively, degrading responsiveness in agentic loops (not a correctness bug, but a reliability/UX one for long-running agent sessions).
- Hacker News commentary (mixed, but leaning positive for agentic use): one user reports better agentic-task experience with GLM-4.6 than "other agentic software including Claude Code, with only Codex coming close"; another confirms GLM-4.7 "handles agentic workflows better than Qwen3 or GPT-OSS at similar sizes" and can run locally on a Mac Studio. These are anecdotal, unverified, and should be weighted as sentiment, not measurement. [HN #45602075, #46357287]

### Gap acknowledgment

Direct Reddit r/LocalLLaMA thread-level reports specifically about GLM-4.6/4.7 *ignoring or drifting from long structured instructions* (as opposed to tool-call format bugs, which are a related but distinct failure mode) were not found despite multiple targeted search attempts in this research pass — this is a genuine scarcity, not a decision to omit. The GitHub issue evidence above is a reasonable proxy (it's the same population of coding-agent power users who would post such reports) but doesn't directly answer "does GLM skip step 4 of a 6-step instruction block."

## 5. MiniMax M2.7

### Official positioning

- MiniMax's own technical report (arXiv 2605.26494, "The MiniMax-M2 Series: Mini Activations Unleashing Max Real-World Intelligence") and product pages position M2.7 for "advanced coding assistance, agentic workflows, long-horizon software engineering, live production troubleshooting... and other complex multi-step productivity tasks" — the "long-horizon" and "multi-step" framing is directly relevant to `hash`'s multi-phase, multi-pass structure. A specific claim: M2.7 can "reduce recovery time for live production incidents to under three minutes on multiple occasions" in SRE-style debugging workflows requiring monitoring, trace analysis, and database verification in sequence — a genuinely relevant multi-step-instruction-adherence use case, though self-reported. [minimax.io/models/text/m27; NVIDIA developer blog]

### Benchmarks

- M2.7: 56.22% SWE-bench Pro, 55.6% VIBE-Pro, 57.0% Terminal-Bench 2, 39.8% NL2Repo, 1495 ELO GDPval-AA, 46.3% Toolathon, 62.7% MM Claw, 66.6% medal rate MLE-Bench Lite. Earlier M2 (non-.7) reported τ²-Bench 77.2, BrowseComp 44.0, FinSearchComp-global 65.5, positioned by VentureBeat as "the new king of open source LLMs, especially for agentic tool calling" at release. [minimax.io; venturebeat.com; arXiv 2605.26494]
- Terminal-Bench and SWE-bench-family scores are the closest proxies available to "does it follow a long procedural task correctly," since both require sustained multi-step tool use inside a bounded task — but neither isolates instruction-*following* from raw coding capability, so a high score is necessary-but-not-sufficient evidence for `hash`'s purposes.
- No IFEval/FollowBench/Multi-IF score specific to MiniMax M2.7 was found in this research pass.

### Community and GitHub-issue evidence (version-matched, directly relevant)

- **vllm issue #44060**: "MiniMax-M2.1/M2.5/M2.7 tool-call arguments cannot safely contain raw `</parameter>` delimiters" — a structural bug where the model's own tool-calling protocol collides with XML-style delimiters that can legitimately appear inside file contents or code being edited, which the issue says users must work around by escaping/encoding arguments before passing them through. This is exactly the kind of failure that would silently corrupt a `hash` apply-phase TDD tool call if the code under test happened to contain that delimiter sequence — a version-matched (explicitly names M2.7), directly relevant finding. [github.com/vllm-project/vllm/issues/44060]
- **sglang issue #23071**: MiniMax-M2 streaming tool_calls return malformed responses (`name: null`, duplicated arguments) under certain streaming conditions — a correctness bug in the exact multi-tool-call agentic loop pattern `hash`'s apply phase runs repeatedly (one tool call per RED/GREEN/REFACTOR step, many times per task).
- **sglang issue #16057**: MiniMax M2/M2.1 tool parser fails specifically on `str | null` union-typed parameters — a narrower parsing gap but still directly in the tool-calling correctness path.
- Analyticsvidhya and DataCamp hands-on reviews of local MiniMax M2.7 deployment report favorable subjective impressions ("smaller than GLM 5.1, feels faster, understands code well... even the 4-bit version doing very well on complex tasks," ~120 tok/s) but these are single-reviewer anecdotes, not controlled comparisons.

### Gap acknowledgment

Same gap as GLM: no Reddit r/LocalLLaMA thread was found specifically discussing MiniMax M2/M2.7 *forgetting or drifting from* long structured instructions (as distinct from tool-call format bugs). The GitHub issue trail is the strongest available evidence and is genuinely useful — it shows the tool-calling *substrate* MiniMax models rely on has known, version-matched fragility in exactly the sustained multi-tool-call agentic pattern `hash` uses — but it answers a related, not identical, question to "does the model comply with a 50-line instruction block."

## 6. General, model-agnostic findings

### Prompt-length / instruction-count degradation (measured, not just claimed)

- **Chroma's "Context Rot" research** (2025, tested 18 frontier models incl. GPT-4.1, Claude 4-gen, Gemini 2.5, Qwen3): "model reliability decreases significantly with longer inputs, even on simple tasks like retrieval and text replication... every model degraded with length, no model held flat across its advertised window... a model with a 200K token window can exhibit significant degradation at 50K tokens." Directly falsifies the assumption that "the context window is big enough, so length doesn't matter" — relevant because `hash`'s full artifact set (discovery + proposal + specs + design + tasks, all re-read every align pass per line 174) can itself approach tens of thousands of tokens well before hitting any advertised context ceiling. [research.trychroma.com/context-rot]
- **"Lost in the middle" (Liu et al., and reaffirmed by the Context Rot study)**: a U-shaped attention curve — accuracy highest when relevant content is at the start or end of context, with 30+ percentage-point drops for content in the middle third (positions 5–15 of a 20-item context in the original study's framing). This is the single most load-bearing, most-replicated finding in this entire research pass, cited independently by Anthropic's own docs (§3), Chroma's cross-model study, and multiple practitioner syntheses.
- **AGENTIF (arXiv 2505.16944, NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks Spotlight)** is the most directly relevant benchmark found for `hash`'s use case: it evaluates instruction-following specifically in *agentic* scenarios, built from 707 human-annotated real instructions across 50 real agentic applications, averaging **1,723 words and 11.9 simultaneous constraints per instruction** — a close structural match to `hash`'s own align/apply instruction blocks (which run 40-50 lines and combine sequencing, severity classification, tagging, and conditional branching in one block). Its headline finding: **models drop dramatically versus their own IFEval scores once constraint count rises** — e.g. GPT-4o drops from 87.0 (IFEval) to 58.5 (AGENTIF) — and "current models generally perform poorly, especially in handling complex constraint structures and tool specifications." This is the strongest available evidence that instruction-following degrades as a function of *constraint count*, independent of raw token length, and it's bad news specifically for instruction blocks shaped like `hash`'s align/apply phases (which stack sequencing + severity classification + tag-based branching + a stop condition all in one prompt). [arxiv.org/abs/2505.16944; github.com/THU-KEG/AgentIF]

### Structured format effects (XML/markdown vs prose)

- No single controlled study with a clean before/after compliance percentage for "XML tags vs markdown vs prose" was found in this pass (this remains one of the more asserted-not-measured claims in the prompt-engineering literature generally). What is well-supported is the *directional* claim, repeated across Anthropic's own docs and independent practitioner guides: structuring critical content with clear delimiters (headers, tags, numbered lists) helps a model locate and weight instructions correctly, especially combined with placement discipline (start/end, not middle). `hash`'s existing structure (YAML block scalars with markdown-style bold/numbered-list instruction bodies) already follows this shape; no source found argues for switching to raw XML tags specifically.

### Self-review vs fresh-context review (direct empirical support for `hash`'s subagent-review pattern)

- **"Cross-Context Review" (arXiv 2603.12123)** is the most directly relevant paper found in this entire research pass: it tests four review conditions — same-session self-review, repeated self-review, context-aware subagent review, and cross-context review (fresh session, no access to production history) — on 30 artifacts with 150 injected errors. Result: **cross-context review achieved F1 28.6% vs. same-session self-review's 24.6%** — a measured, if modest, improvement. The paper's mechanism explanation is directly relevant to *why* `hash` mandates a fresh subagent for apply-phase review (line 220): "the reviewer does not know it produced the artifact, so sycophancy and self-preference bias lose their trigger... a fresh session has no prior decisions to anchor to, which prevents confirmation loops." The paper also flags a limit worth noting for `hash`: fresh context removes *context-level* error correlation but not *model-level* correlated failure modes (i.e., a fresh Claude subagent reviewing Claude-generated code can still share the same blind spots baked into the model's training, even without conversational bias) — a genuine ceiling, not a reason to abandon the pattern. [arxiv.org/html/2603.12123]
- A related paper ("More Rounds, More Noise: Why Multi-Turn Review Fails to Improve Cross-Context Verification," arXiv 2603.16244) is a useful caution for `hash`'s 3-pass loop structure: the title alone suggests that simply adding more review rounds doesn't monotonically improve outcomes and can add noise — this cuts in favor of `hash`'s early-stop-on-clean-pass design (lines 171, 196, 219, 252) over an unconditional fixed-N-passes design, since the paper implies more rounds isn't free.

### Stop conditions / loop termination

- Recent (2026) agentic-systems research explicitly names the failure class `hash`'s loop design is built to avoid: **"Infinite Agentic Loops"** (arXiv 2607.01641) — "structural execution failures in which agentic feedback paths repeatedly trigger... execution without an effective termination condition." The same literature converges on a specific, actionable claim: **"external, objective, and deterministic mechanisms that monitor the agent's behavior and enforce strict termination conditions override the agent's internal logic. The system running the agent, not the agent itself, is ultimately responsible for guaranteeing termination."** [dev.to/mukundakatta on llm-stop-conditions; arXiv 2607.01641]
- A more adversarial framing — **"Termination Poisoning"** (arXiv 2605.05846) — describes attacks where an agent's self-assessed progress signal is corrupted by external content, causing it to falsely believe a stop condition is or isn't met. Not directly an attack surface for `hash` (schema.yaml isn't processing adversarial external content in the review loop), but the underlying mechanism — an LLM's self-report of "is this pass clean?" being the sole termination signal — is structurally the same mechanism `hash`'s align/apply loops rely on (lines 171, 196: "stopping early as soon as a pass finds no HIGH and no MEDIUM issues" is entirely determined by the model's own classification of its own findings). The literature's consistent recommendation is to back self-reported completion with an external, deterministic check where one is available. `hash` already partially does this for align (line 174: "also run `openspec validate --strict` for deterministic structural checks") but apply's REVIEW phase stop condition (line 252) rests entirely on the fresh subagent's own HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW self-classification with no external verification step.

### Severity/priority-tag compliance and conditional branching

- The most directly relevant source found (a practitioner blog on classification-prompt design) states plainly: "when defining severity-priority rules for classification, you should document the logic explicitly in your prompt, and the model will follow it consistently if you state it clearly" — an assertion consistent with, but not independently verified beyond, general prompt-engineering practice. No controlled study quantifying "compliance rate on HIGH vs LOW branching instructions" specifically was found.
- AGENTIF's finding that models "generally perform poorly... in handling complex constraint structures" (§ above) is the closest available quantified evidence that conditional/branching instructions (of which severity-tag-driven branching is one instance) are harder for current models than linear ones — this is an inference from a closely related finding, not a direct measurement of severity-tag compliance specifically, and should be weighted accordingly.

A capitalized STOP buried in the middle of a 50-line instruction block is weaker, per the "lost in the middle" evidence, than the same word placed at a natural end-of-block boundary — which is already schema.yaml's actual pattern (both STOP instances, lines 196 and 252, sit at the end of their instruction blocks, not the middle). | No new rewording evidenced — the existing placement (STOP/DO NOT at block boundaries, not mid-block) already matches what the literature recommends independent of capitalization. |

## 8. Recommendation

**No schema.yaml edit is proposed by this document** (per the pure-research constraint), but the evidence supports three observations worth carrying into any future, separately-scoped change:

1. **The two patterns this research evidences most strongly — fresh-subagent review and early-stop-on-clean loops — are already in schema.yaml and already shaped the way the evidence recommends.** No wording change is warranted for align's or apply's loop structure or the subagent-delegation requirement.
2. **The one concrete, evidenced gap is apply-REVIEW's stop condition (line 252) lacking a deterministic backstop equivalent to align's `openspec validate --strict` (line 174).** The stop-condition literature (§6) is consistent that self-reported "no HIGH/MEDIUM issues" completion should be backed by an external, deterministic check where one exists — and one already exists here (the test suite, referenced at line 222 as something the review "runs," but not wired into the actual stop/continue decision at line 252). This is a narrow, evidence-backed candidate for a future proposal, not something this document applies.
3. **Instruction density in align/apply (§7's last row) is the one place the general literature (AGENTIF specifically) suggests real, measured risk** — not from any wording flaw, but from stacking ~6+ simultaneous classification/sequencing/reporting demands into one instruction block, which is close to the constraint count AGENTIF shows causes the largest compliance drop versus simpler instructions. This is flagged as a DECISION-tier consideration for schema maintainers (worth a future dedicated research or design pass on whether splitting align/apply's instruction into an explicit sequential checklist would help) — not a recommendation this document is positioned to resolve, since no controlled study isolating that specific intervention on a schema.yaml-shaped prompt was found.
4. **No model-specific fork of schema.yaml is supported by this research.** Nothing found suggests Claude, GLM, or MiniMax diverge enough on the patterns `hash` actually uses (loop termination, severity tagging, fresh-context review) to warrant different instruction wording per model. The clearest per-model risk found — GLM-4.7's and MiniMax-M2.7's version-matched tool-call-format GitHub issues (§4, §5) — is an inference-stack/serving-layer reliability question (would a malformed tool call break a `hash` TDD cycle), not an instruction-comprehension question, and sits outside schema.yaml's own text entirely.
